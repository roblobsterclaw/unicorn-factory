The Problem — 48 Million Americans Get Denied Every Year

📊 The Numbers Are Staggering

In 2021, the 162 major medical insurers on HealthCare.gov received 291.6 million in-network claims and denied 48.3 million of them — a 16.6% denial rate. Some insurers denied nearly HALF of all claims (Meridian Health Plan: ~49%).

The total US insurance market is far larger: when you add employer plans, Medicaid managed care, Medicare Advantage, auto, and home insurance, the total denied claims universe exceeds 200 million per year.

The brutal truth: Fewer than 2 out of 1,000 denied claims are ever appealed. Insurers count on this. Denial is a profit strategy.

😤 The Emotional Pain Point

You're already sick or your house just flooded. Now you get a dense 8-page denial letter written in legalese. You don't know what "medical necessity" means in your plan's definition. You don't know what "referral requirement" means in your specific policy. You don't know the appeals deadline (typically 180 days for health, 60-90 for property).

So you give up. The insurance company wins. They keep $3,000–$15,000 that was rightfully yours.

ClaimPilot converts that giving-up moment into a 60-second submission.

The Solution — How ClaimPilot Works
1

Upload the Denial Letter

Consumer photos or PDFs their denial letter. OCR + LLM extracts the claim number, denial reason, insurer, amount, and deadline. 60 seconds of work.

2

Upload the Insurance Policy (or Link EOB)

Consumer provides their policy PDF or connects to their insurer portal via OAuth. AI reads the policy definitions, exclusions, and appeals process. Also analyzes the Explanation of Benefits (EOB) for discrepancies.

3

AI Diagnoses the Denial

The AI categorizes the denial type, identifies likely grounds for reversal (billing code error, missing auth that was waived, policy language ambiguity, ACA protections), and assigns a confidence score to the appeal.

4

Draft the Appeal Letter

AI generates a professionally written appeal citing: (a) specific policy language, (b) applicable federal laws (ACA, ERISA, No Surprises Act), (c) state insurance regulations, (d) clinical guidelines if medical necessity. Attorney-quality output.

5

Consumer Reviews & Submits

Consumer reads the draft, approves it (or edits it), and ClaimPilot handles submission via certified mail template or direct portal submission. System tracks the appeal deadline and follows up automatically.

6

ClaimPilot Collects on Success

If the appeal is overturned, ClaimPilot collects 15–20% of the recovered amount. No recovery = no fee. Subscriber plan ($29/mo) covers ongoing monitoring and unlimited denials. Consumer wins either way.

The Gallagher Parallel: This is the same model as insurance brokers and public adjusters — complicated process, done FOR you, percentage of outcome. Except ClaimPilot does it in 5 minutes with AI vs. weeks with a human.

90-Day Startup Plan

⚠️ Critical Legal Question First: Do You Need a Public Adjuster License?

This is the most important question before you build anything. Public adjuster licensing is required in most states for negotiating insurance settlements on behalf of consumers for property claims (home, auto). Health insurance appeals have a different legal framework.

✅ Health Appeals (Lower Barrier)

No public adjuster license required. ACA mandates consumer appeal rights. Drafting appeal letters for health claims is generally legal as long as you're not "practicing law." Structure as a technology service, not legal representation. Works in all 50 states.

⚠️ Property Claims (Higher Barrier)

Public adjuster license required in 47 states for property claims. Licenses are state-by-state, require exam and background check, typically $300-500 each. NJ license required. START with health, add property after you're established.

Section 1202 Structure — QSBS Tax Exclusion
The Big Number: If ClaimPilot sells for $50M, Joe could potentially exclude $10M+ of capital gains from federal tax entirely. Section 1202 Qualified Small Business Stock is the single best legal tax advantage available to startup founders.

✅ ClaimPilot Qualifies Because:

  • C-corporation (required — not LLC, not S-corp)
  • Incorporated in US (Delaware)
  • At time of investment, aggregate gross assets under $50M
  • Technology/software company (qualifies — insurance is excluded, but software tools serving insurance qualify)
  • Stock held for 5+ years triggers exclusion
  • Stock acquired at original issuance (not secondary market)

⚠️ Watch Out For:

  • Insurance companies themselves are excluded from QSBS — ClaimPilot is a tech company, not an insurer, so this should be fine
  • Financial services businesses excluded — ensure ClaimPilot is positioned as "technology" not "financial service"
  • 5-year holding period required for maximum exclusion (10x or $10M, whichever is greater)
  • State-level exclusions vary — NJ may not fully conform

Recommended Structure

1

Delaware C-Corp

ClaimPilot Inc. — incorporated in Delaware, day one. This is non-negotiable for QSBS.

2

Issue Founder Stock Immediately

Joe receives 10M shares at $0.0001 par value. Total consideration: ~$1,000. This is your QSBS position — start the 5-year clock.

3

83(b) Election — File Within 30 Days!

If any stock vests over time, file an 83(b) election with the IRS within 30 days of grant. Missing this is a $100,000+ mistake. Non-negotiable.

4

Keep Gross Assets Under $50M

Before you take outside investment, ensure the company's assets plus any investment don't push over $50M. This threshold must be met at time of stock issuance for each investor to qualify.

Risk Analysis — Charlie Munger Inversion: How Does ClaimPilot Fail?
Munger's Rule: "Invert, always invert. Tell me where I'll die so I never go there." Here are the ways ClaimPilot dies — and how to avoid each one.
⚖️

Risk #1: Unauthorized Practice of Law (Highest Priority)

If ClaimPilot drafts appeal letters and a state bar association decides that's "practicing law," the company faces cease-and-desist orders and potential criminal charges. Mitigation: Structure as document assistance (like LegalZoom), not legal advice. Partner with licensed attorneys in each state for the premium tier. Get an insurance law attorney opinion BEFORE launch.

🏛️

Risk #2: State Regulatory Action

Insurance commissioners could classify ClaimPilot as a public adjuster or claims management company requiring licensing. Some states are aggressive regulators. Mitigation: Start in NJ + federal-only health claims (ACA plans). Build relationships with insurance regulators early. Get licensed as a public adjuster where required.

🏢

Risk #3: Insurance Company Retaliation

A major insurer could change their appeals process to require attorney representation, add technical barriers, or lobby state legislatures to restrict AI-generated appeals. Mitigation: Focus on ACA federal protections where insurers have limited ability to restrict appeals. Scale fast before insurers notice.

🤖

Risk #4: AI Accuracy and Liability

If ClaimPilot's AI gives bad advice that causes a consumer to miss their appeal deadline or file an incorrect appeal, ClaimPilot could face lawsuits. Mitigation: Robust disclaimers, E&O insurance ($2M minimum), human review tier for large claims ($5K+), and build in deadline tracking so no consumer misses their window.

💻

Risk #5: BigTech Enters the Space

Google has all the search data, AI capability, and consumer trust to build this in 6 months. If Google Health or a well-funded startup decides to go after this market, they could out-resource ClaimPilot. Mitigation: Scale fast, build the data moat, establish brand trust, and make yourself a compelling acquisition target (this is likely the exit path anyway).

📉

Risk #6: Collection is Harder Than It Looks

Getting consumers to pay 15-20% of their recovered amount after the fact requires a solid contract and willingness to pursue non-payers. Mitigation: Require credit card on file before starting appeal. Process payment automatically when insurer confirms payment. Partner with payment processors that handle escrow-like timing.
